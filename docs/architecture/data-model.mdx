This page walks through the core entities in the platform, what they represent, and how they connect. It's meant to give you a mental model of the system — whether you're building an integration or just getting oriented.

## Customers and accounts

Everything starts with a **Customer**. A customer represents an end-user — typically the person or entity on whose behalf your platform operates. Each customer owns one or more **Accounts**, and accounts are where nearly all activity happens: trading, payments, holdings, billing, and more.

Think of the customer as the identity layer and the account as the operational layer.

<Frame>
```
Customer
 └── Account
      ├── Positions (holdings)
      ├── Cash Balances
      ├── Orders (trading)
      ├── Deposits & Withdrawals (payments)
      ├── Funding Accounts
      ├── Transactions
      ├── Valuation & Valuation History
      ├── Earnings (performance)
      └── Fee Charges (billing)
```
</Frame>

Accounts are the central resource — most API endpoints are scoped to an account. For example, you fetch positions, place orders, and initiate payments all under `/accounts/{account_id}/...`.

Accounts can also be looked up by your own external reference in addition to our internal ID, making it easy to map between your system and ours.

## Catalog: instruments and prices

The **Catalog** is our shared reference data. It contains two key resources:

- **Instruments** — the financial products available on the platform (stocks, ETFs, funds, etc.). Each instrument has a unique ID and a symbol. You can look up an instrument by either.
- **Prices** — the latest market prices for instruments. We use prices for order validation, valuation, and performance calculations.

The catalog is read-only from your perspective — we maintain the universe of instruments and their pricing data.

## Trading

When you place a trade on behalf of a customer, you create an **Order** on their account. An order represents the intent to buy or sell a specific quantity of an instrument.

Orders go through a lifecycle. They start as **Pending Orders** (visible via a dedicated endpoint) and eventually resolve — either filling successfully or being cancelled. Key error scenarios include:

- The instrument isn't currently tradable (`instrument_not_trading`)
- The account has insufficient funds or units (`insufficient_funds`, `insufficient_units`)
- Buy or sell orders aren't enabled for the account (`buy_order_forbidden`, `sell_order_forbidden`)

When orders fill, the account's **Positions** and **Cash Balances** update to reflect the new holdings.

## Holdings

An account's holdings consist of two things:

- **Positions** — the instruments the account currently holds, along with quantity and cost information. Each position is tied to a specific instrument from the catalog.
- **Cash Balances** — the monetary balances in the account, potentially across multiple currencies.

Together, positions and cash balances represent the full picture of what an account owns.

## Payments: deposits and withdrawals

Money moves in and out of accounts through **Deposits** and **Withdrawals**.

Before a customer can make a payment, they need at least one **Funding Account** — typically a bank account identified by an IBAN and bank code. Funding accounts are linked to the customer's account and serve as the source for deposits and the destination for withdrawals.

Payments go through their own lifecycle and can fail if, for example, withdrawals aren't enabled for the account (`withdrawal_forbidden`) or a deposit is rejected (`deposit_forbidden`).

Deposits can also support an advance mechanism (when enabled), allowing the account to trade before the deposit settles. Refunds are available on payments up to the refundable amount.

## Valuation and performance

- **Valuation** gives you the current total value of an account, combining positions (at current market prices) and cash balances into a single figure.
- **Valuation History** provides historical snapshots so you can see how account value has changed over time.
- **Earnings** are the performance metrics for an account — how much the account has gained or lost.

These are all computed resources. You read them, but we calculate them based on positions, prices, and transaction history.

## Billing

We track billing through **Fee Charges**. A fee charge represents a specific billing event applied to an account — you can look up individual fee charges by ID.

## Files and legal documents

We have a lightweight document management system built around two concepts:

- **Files** — uploaded via a two-step process. First, you request a **Signed Upload** URL, upload the file content directly to cloud storage, and then register the file with our API. This keeps large file content off the API itself.
- **Legal Documents** — represent versioned legal agreements (terms of service, disclosures, etc.). Each legal document is created with its first version and can have multiple **Legal Document Versions** over time. Every version points to a file. You can always retrieve the latest version's file directly, or fetch a specific historical version.

<Note>
A legal document always has at least one version — we create the document and its first version in a single transaction, so there's no such thing as an empty legal document without content.
</Note>

## Events and webhooks

Whenever something meaningful happens (an order fills, a payment completes, etc.), we create an **Event**. Events have a name (e.g., `order.filled`), a reference to the resource that changed, and event-specific data.

You can poll for events via the API, but the real-time path is **Webhooks**. A webhook is a URL you register with us. When events fire, we create a **Webhook Delivery** for each active webhook subscription and `POST` the event payload to your endpoint.

Deliveries are tracked with their own lifecycle (`pending` → `in_progress` → `succeeded` or `failed`) and are retried automatically if your endpoint doesn't respond with a `2xx`. For full details on delivery mechanics, retries, and deduplication, see the [Webhooks documentation](/docs/webhooks/overview).

## How it all connects

Here's a summary of the key relationships:

| Parent | Child | Relationship |
|---|---|---|
| Customer | Account | A customer has one or more accounts |
| Account | Position | An account holds zero or more positions |
| Account | Cash Balance | An account has one or more cash balances (by currency) |
| Account | Order | Orders are placed on an account |
| Account | Deposit / Withdrawal | Payments move money in and out of an account |
| Account | Funding Account | Bank accounts linked for payments |
| Account | Transaction | A ledger of all financial activity on the account |
| Account | Valuation | Computed current value of the account |
| Account | Earnings | Computed performance metrics |
| Account | Fee Charge | Billing events on the account |
| Instrument | Position | Each position references an instrument |
| Instrument | Price | Prices are published per instrument |
| Instrument | Order | Each order targets an instrument |
| Legal Document | Legal Document Version | A document has one or more versions |
| Legal Document Version | File | Each version references an uploaded file |
| Event | Webhook Delivery | One delivery per event per active webhook |
| Webhook | Webhook Delivery | Deliveries are dispatched to a webhook endpoint |

## ID conventions

Every resource in the system has a unique ID. IDs are prefixed with a short resource identifier (e.g., `evt_` for events, `ord_` for orders, `whd_` for webhook deliveries). Treat IDs as opaque strings — don't parse or make assumptions about their format. See [Best practices](/api-reference/best-practices) for more on working with IDs.
